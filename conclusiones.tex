This study presented a rigorous experimental characterization of Body Shadowing effects on 6.5 GHz UWB positioning, addressing the gap in empirical data for this frequency band. The following core conclusions are drawn:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Optimal Tag Placement:} The head is unequivocally the most robust location for human-centric UWB applications, yielding an indoor NLOS accuracy of 18.16 cm, compared to 95.11 cm for the hip. This establishes a design constraint for safety-critical systems: reliance on belt-mounted tags introduces unacceptable error risks without auxiliary compensation.
    
    \item \textbf{Frequency-Specific Propagation:} We identified that at 6.5 GHz, the indoor environment acts as a "diversity provider," reducing NLOS errors compared to outdoor environments for diffractive body locations. This "Constructive Multipath" effect is a distinct feature that can be exploited in indoor network planning.
    
    \item \textbf{Statistical Error Modeling:} The assumption of Gaussian noise is invalid for human tracking in NLOS. We demonstrated that errors follow a Log-normal distribution with heavy tails. Consequently, standard Extended Kalman Filters fail to correct the systematic positive bias induced by the body, acting only as smoothers rather than correctors.
    
    \item \textbf{System Viability:} Despite body shadowing challenges, a standard UWB system at 6.5 GHz achieves sub-meter accuracy (55-95 cm) even in worst-case scenarios (hip placement), which remains sufficient for room-level localization, though insufficient for precision navigation without advanced algorithms.
\end{enumerate}

Future work will focus on developing a hybrid "bias-aware" EKF that utilizes real-time ToF variance to dynamically switch between LOS (Gaussian) and NLOS (Log-normal) observation models, directly addressing the limitations identified in this study.
