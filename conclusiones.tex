This work presented an exhaustive experimental characterization of the body shadowing effect in a UWB positioning system operating at 6.5 GHz. It has been quantitatively demonstrated that the location of the device on the body is a critical factor that can degrade distance estimation accuracy by more than an order of magnitude.

The main conclusions of this study indicate that the location on the head provides the greatest robustness and consistency against body obstruction, while the hip and chest turn out to be the positions most vulnerable to significant errors in Non-Line-of-Sight conditions. Likewise, it was determined that the error induced by body obstruction in NLOS conditions does not follow a Gaussian distribution, which highlights the need to employ localization and filtering algorithms capable of handling outliers. On the other hand, the results confirm that the 6.5 GHz band is fully viable for human tracking, achieving a 2D positioning accuracy of between 55 and 95 cm. Finally, it was observed that multipath indoors can generate a constructive effect that improves accuracy in obstruction conditions compared to open outdoor environments.

The contributions of this research include an experimental database for the 6.5 GHz band, practical design guidelines for commercial systems, and performance benchmarks that can serve as a reference for future work.

As future work, it is recommended to extend the analysis to a larger number of participants to model anthropometric variability, evaluate more complex and dynamic scenarios, and develop advanced mitigation algorithms that fuse UWB data with inertial sensors to improve system robustness and precision.
