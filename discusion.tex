\subsection{Impact of Body Location and Propagation Geometry}

The results obtained confirm that device location on the body is a determining factor in the accuracy of the UWB positioning system at 6.5 GHz. The head location demonstrated superior robustness, attributable to its convex geometry which facilitates wave diffraction around the surface, maintaining signal integrity and allowing predictable delays even in Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS) conditions. In contrast, torso locations (chest and hip) suffered severe degradations, with errors increasing significantly (degradation factors greater than 10x). This is explained by the large cross-section of the torso and the water-rich biological composition, which effectively block the direct signal, forcing measurement through longer reflected paths or attenuated signals. These findings corroborate and quantify for the 6.5 GHz band phenomena previously observed at lower frequencies \cite{ref4, ref16, ref14, ref15}.

Additionally, a notable differential behavior was observed where certain locations presented more robust connectivity indoors than outdoors under NLOS conditions. This suggests that, in confined environments, the presence of multiple reflective surfaces facilitates "constructive multipath". Although the direct signal is blocked by the body, reflections from walls and the ceiling allow maintaining service availability, unlike the outdoor scenario where the absence of nearby reflectors results in total signal loss. This finding is relevant for the design of UWB systems in dense industrial environments.

\subsection{Error Nature and Filtering Performance}

A critical finding of this study is the non-Gaussian and biased nature of the error under severe body obstruction conditions. Standard localization algorithms, such as the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), implicitly assume that measurement noise follows a normal distribution with zero mean ($\mathcal{N}(0, R)$). However, our statistical analyses demonstrate that body obstruction introduces a systematic positive bias (measured distance is consistently greater than real distance due to multipath delay) and an error distribution that fits better to Log-normal models.

The EKF performance analysis revealed that, in the absence of obstruction or with mild obstruction (head), the filter operates correctly by smoothing random noise. However, in severe obstruction scenarios (hip), the filter converges towards an incorrect but smooth trajectory. This is due to a \textit{model mismatch}: the filter interprets the systematic bias as the true position, since it lacks *a priori* information to distinguish between a real displacement and a constant measurement error. This demonstrates that the standard EKF acts effectively as a trajectory smoother, but is ineffective for correcting bias errors due to NLOS without auxiliary mechanisms.

\subsection{Implications and Limitations}

For practical applications, these results imply that the choice of \textit{tag} location is a critical design decision. For applications requiring high precision and robustness, the head or high locations on the body are preferable. If the device must be worn on the torso, it is imperative to implement NLOS mitigation algorithms that can detect blocking conditions through indicators such as time-of-flight variance or signal power, and compensate for the bias before the filtering stage.

Finally, it is important to mention the limitations of the study, such as the use of a limited number of test subjects (three) and a simplified geometric scenario. Future research should address data collection with a larger number of subjects to generalize anthropometric effects and explore real-time adaptive bias estimation techniques to improve accuracy in critical locations such as the hip.
