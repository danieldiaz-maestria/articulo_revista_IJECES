\subsection{Physical Determinants of Device Placement Reliability}

The experimental findings fundamentally challenge the "one-size-fits-all" approach to UWB tag placement. For the 6.5 GHz band, the severe impact of body shadowing is not merely an attenuation factor (scaling noise), but a complex geometric dependency that structurally alters the underlying probability distribution of the error. 

The superior performance of the \textbf{head location} under obstructed conditions (MAE $\approx$ 18.6 cm in NLOS compared to $\approx$ 97.7 cm for the hip) dictates that industrial safety and tracking applications must prioritize helmet-mounted or high-shoulder placements over belt-mounted alternatives, despite potential ergonomic trade-offs. The 6.5 GHz frequency presents a wavelength of approximately 4.6 cm, which enables a strong "creeping wave" diffraction effect around the curved geometry of the human skull, allowing the signal to wrap around the physical obstruction efficiently without incurring catastrophic delay.

Conversely, the massive deterioration observed in torso locations (\textbf{chest and hip}) stems from complete physical occlusion. The torso functions as an insurmountable dielectric wall. When line-of-sight is blocked by the torso mass, the receiver is entirely reliant on weak, multi-path reflections that take significantly longer paths to reach the destination. The difference of nearly a full meter in error under NLOS between the head and the hip provides empirical proof that placing UWB trackers on a standard waist belt introduces unacceptable safety margins for precision applications.

\subsection{The "Constructive Multipath" Paradox}

A counter-intuitive but critical discovery of this investigation is the "Constructive Multipath" effect observed in the indoor corridor scenario. Traditional RF theory posits that confined indoor environments inherently exhibit poorer positioning performance due to multi-path fading and interference, while open outdoor fields represent the ideal propagation scenario.

However, our empirical data reveals a paradox for highly mobile body locations. By performing a paired non-parametric comparison (Mann-Whitney U) between outdoor and indoor NLOS measurements for the same extremities, we found that for the \textbf{head, hand, wrist, knee, and ankle}, the median distance error was consistently and significantly \textit{lower} indoors than outdoors. For example, the hand location exhibited a large effect size ($r = -0.693$, $p \ll 0.001$) favoring the confined corridor environment. 

The physical mechanism driving this paradox is constructive multi-path. In a completely open outdoor field, when the human body blocks the direct line-of-sight path, there are no environmental reflectors available to route the signal to the anchor. Consequently, the signal experiences massive attenuation, pushing the receiver to lock onto highly distorted ground reflections or failing entirely. However, in an indoor corridor, the walls, ceiling, and floor act as a dense reflective network. These surfaces generate multiple strong indirect trajectories that circumvent the biological blockage. For extremities that constantly change spatial orientation (like the hand or wrist), these diverse reflective paths "rescue" the signal, resulting in a significantly lower overall delay than the outdoor "pure obstruction" case. This validates that indoor clutter can serve as a beneficial spatial diversity provider for 6.5 GHz UWB systems under body shadowing constraints.

\subsection{Failure of Gaussian Assumptions in Parametric Models}

The most consequential theoretical finding of this study is the definitive statistical proof that ranging errors generated by 6.5 GHz Body Shadowing do not adhere to Normal (Gaussian) distributions. As rigorously demonstrated in Section \ref{sec:results}, the error distribution undergoes a structural transformation from a Gamma distribution in LOS to a heavy-tailed Log-normal distribution in NLOS.

This fundamentally explains the persistent failure of standard positioning algorithms, including standard Extended Kalman Filters (EKF) and Least Squares multilateration, to correct Body Shadowing biases. Traditional state-estimation filters explicitly formulate their measurement update models assuming zero-mean Gaussian noise ($\mathcal{N}(0, R)$). However, the Log-normal distribution of NLOS errors implies a massive positive skewness and a non-zero systematic bias. 

When a standard filter processes a Log-normal noise profile, it interprets the systematic time-delay introduced by the body as a legitimate physical displacement of the user. Consequently, the filter fails to correct the position and instead mathematically smooths the error, resulting in a trajectory that drifts smoothly away from reality. 

These findings establish an undeniable empirical baseline for the future design of UWB positioning systems: traditional Gaussian-based solvers are fundamentally flawed for human-centric tracking. Future systems require dynamic, "distribution-aware" architectures capable of detecting the onset of NLOS conditions (e.g., via ToF variance or signal-to-noise ratio) and autonomously switching the measurement observation model from a Gaussian/Gamma assumption to a heavy-tailed Log-normal bias compensation model in real-time.
