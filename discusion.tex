\subsection{Physical Determinants of Device Placement Reliability}

The experimental findings fundamentally challenge the "one-size-fits-all" approach to UWB tag placement. For the 6.5 GHz band, the severe impact of BS is not only due to signal attenuation; it is primarily manifested as a delay-estimation bias that structurally alters the underlying probability distribution of the ranging error.

The superior performance of head placement under BS conditions (MAE $\approx$ 18.6 cm in NLOS compared to $\approx$ 97.7 cm for the hip) indicates that industrial safety and tracking applications should prioritize helmet-mounted or high-shoulder placements over belt-mounted alternatives, despite possible ergonomic trade-offs. This aligns with the findings of Otim \textit{et al.} \cite{ref4} \cite{Otim2019}, who identified the head (forehead) as the optimal placement, reporting minimal errors ($\approx$ 13 to 35 cm) due to a higher probability of LOS. The 6.5 GHz frequency presents a wavelength of approximately 4.6 cm, which enables a pronounced "creeping wave" diffraction effect around the curved geometry of the human skull. The physical dimension of the skull (approximately 20 cm) provides a sufficiently large curvature radius, such that diffraction effects are particularly pronounced at 6.5 GHz relative to lower-frequency bands, where the obstacle-to-wavelength ratio is less favorable for efficient wave bending around the human body. This propagation mechanism, explicitly recognized by Otim \textit{et al.} \cite{Otim2019}, is key to enabling the signal to efficiently wrap around the human body without affecting the ToF measurement.

In contrast, the massive deterioration observed in the placement of the torso, i.e., chest and hip, stems from complete physical occlusion. The torso functions as an insurmountable dielectric wall. When the torso produces BS, the receiver relies on delayed reflected paths, which increases ToF estimation bias and therefore ranging error. Attenuation contributes to this effect, but the dominant observable outcome in this work is the positive ranging bias, frequently adding delays of 0.2 to 0.5 m due to tissue transmission, consistent with observations reported by Tian \textit{et al.} \cite{ref16} and Otim \textit{et al.} \cite{ref4}. The nearly full-meter error under NLOS between the head and the hip provides empirical evidence that placing tags on a standard waist belt introduces unacceptable safety margins for localization precision.

\subsection{The Constructive Multipath Phenomenon}

A counterintuitive but critical discovery of this investigation is the \textit{Constructive Multipath} effect observed in the indoor corridor scenario. Traditional RF theory posits that confined indoor environments inherently exhibit poorer positioning performance due to multipath fading and interference, while open outdoor fields represent the ideal propagation scenario. This is because, in indoor environments, signal transmission is significantly affected by NLOS conditions and multipath interference, which degrade ranging accuracy \cite{WANG2025101979}.

However, the empirical data reveal a paradox for highly mobile body placements. By performing a paired non-parametric comparison, i.e., Mann-Whitney U, between outdoor and indoor NLOS measurements for the same extremities, it was found that for the head, hand, wrist, knee, and ankle, the MAE ranging error was consistently and significantly \textit{lower} indoors than outdoors. For example, the hand placement exhibited a large effect size, i.e., $r = -0.693$, $p \ll 0.001$, favoring the confined corridor environment.

The physical propagation mechanism driving this paradox is constructive multi-path. In a completely open outdoor field, when BS affects the direct LOS path, there are few alternative paths, and the receiver tends to estimate distance from weak, delayed components or fail to track robustly. The outdoor NLOS results presented in Section \ref{sec:results} clearly reveal this loss of ranging capability: measurements become severely degraded and eventually cease to be recorded beyond approximately 6 m for torso placements, i.e., chest and hip. The absence of reflective surfaces directly causes this measurement loss; unlike the indoor corridor, where environmental geometry provides compensatory multipath energy, outdoor NLOS geometry offers no such alternative. Even for favorable placements such as the wrist, outdoor NLOS MAE increases to 24.07 cm compared to 22.00 cm indoors, demonstrating that open-field conditions universally exacerbate BS effects.

Conversely, in an indoor corridor, the walls, ceiling, and floor act as a dense reflective network that can provide earlier usable components for ToF estimation in some body postures. Welch \textit{et al.} \cite{ref15} noted that while the body can attenuate signals severely, i.e., up to 20-30 dB, indoor multipath is typically viewed as a source of degradation via "false first paths". Yet, the findings nuance this view: as suggested by Schmitt \textit{et al.} \cite{Schimtt2019} and Pradabphon \textit{et al.} \cite{Pradabphon2019}, architectural structures such as corridors channel energy and provide alternative paths that maintain connectivity. For extremities that constantly change spatial orientation, i.e., the hand or wrist, these diverse reflective paths can reduce effective ToF bias compared to outdoor BS without useful reflectors. 
\subsection{Failure of Gaussian Assumptions in Parametric Models} The most consequential finding from this study is the definitive statistical evidence that ranging errors generated by 6.5 GHz BS do not follow a normal (Gaussian) distribution. As shown in Section \ref{sec:results}, the error distribution undergoes a structural transformation from a gamma distribution in LOS to a heavy-tailed log-normal distribution in NLOS.

This fundamentally explains the persistent failure of standard positioning algorithms, including Extended Kalman Filters (EKF) and Least Squares multilateration, to correct BS biases. Traditional state-estimation filters explicitly formulate their measurement update models assuming zero-mean Gaussian noise ($\mathcal{N}(0, R)$). However, the log-normal distribution of NLOS errors implies a massive positive skewness and a non-zero systematic bias. As argued by Mirama \textit{et al.} \cite{Mirama2021}, an EKF processing this heavy-tailed, non-zero-mean noise subestimates the error and converges to a displaced position. Similarly, Fan \cite{Fan2019} shows that least squares methods are invalidated by the outliers introduced during BS.

These findings establish an undeniable empirical baseline for the future design of UWB positioning systems: traditional Gaussian-based solvers are fundamentally flawed for human-centric tracking. Future systems require dynamic, "distribution-aware" architectures. Tanghe \textit{et al.} \cite{Tanghe2023} and Mirama \textit{et al.} \cite{ref20} have already pioneered the use of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) and particle filters to handle this multimodal nature. Building on that, the empirical characterization suggests that systems must be capable of detecting the onset of NLOS conditions and autonomously switching the measurement observation model from a Gaussian/gamma assumption to a heavy-tailed log-normal bias-compensation model in real time.
