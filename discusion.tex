\subsection{Body Location as a Determinant of Reliability}
The findings of this study fundamentally challenge the "one-size-fits-all" approach to UWB tag placement. We demonstrated that for the 6.5 GHz band, the impact of body shadowing is not merely an attenuation factor but a geometric dependency that alters the error distribution itself. The superior performance of the head location (MAE $\approx$ 18 cm in NLOS) vs. the hip (MAE $\approx$ 95 cm) suggests that industrial safety applications must prioritize helmet-mounted tags over belt-mounted ones, despite the ergonomic preference for the latter. This 5-fold difference in accuracy is critical for geofencing applications where safety margins are tight.

\subsection{The "Constructive Multipath" Paradox}
A key contribution of this work is the identification of the "Constructive Multipath" effect in indoor NLOS scenarios. Contrary to the intuitive expectation that indoors (with more clutter) would perform worse than outdoors, our results show that for diffractive locations (head, wrist), the indoor environment provides \textit{better} NLOS connectivity. This implies that UWB system designers can leverage environmental reflections to mitigate body shadowing, provided that the receiver sensitivity is sufficient to detect these weaker, reflected paths. This observation is specific to the 6.5 GHz band behavior where specular reflections are more defined than at lower frequencies.

\subsection{Failure of Gaussian Assumptions in EKF}
Our statistical characterization provides strong evidence against the use of standard Gaussian assumptions for human tracking. The clear fit of NLOS errors to Log-normal distributions (Section \ref{sec:results}) explains the failure of standard Extended Kalman Filters (EKF) in correcting position biases. The "smooth but wrong" trajectories observed in the Hip experiments confirm that without an explicit NLOS detection and bias rejection mechanism (e.g., using skewness of ToF or Signal Strength), the EKF interprets the body-induced delay as a real movement. This finding dictates that future UWB positioning algorithms must be "distribution-aware," switching between Gaussian and Log-normal measurement update models based on real-time link diagnostics.

\subsection{Algorithmic Performance: EKF vs. Multilateration}
The comparative analysis between the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) and Least Squares Multilateration highlights a fundamental trade-off in UWB system design. Multilateration, being a memoryless estimator, reacts instantaneously to position changes but is highly susceptible to the "noise spikes" characteristic of NLOS conditions (RMSE degradation > 1 m in Hip scenarios). In contrast, the EKF, acting as a recursive estimator, leverages temporal correlation to smooth out these abrupt errors.

Our implementation demonstrates that the EKF provides a 40-60\% improvement in trajectory consistency (smoothness) compared to raw multilateration. However, this comes at the cost of "inertia": when the bias is systematic (as in the Hip case), the EKF tends to drift smoothly away from the true path rather than jumping erratically. This confirms that while the EKF is superior for user experience (smooth tracks), it cannot inherently correct the Log-normal bias of Body Shadowing without an external correction loop or Non-Line-of-Sight detection mechanism.

\subsection{Implications and Limitations}

For practical applications, these results imply that the choice of \textit{tag} location is a critical design decision. For applications requiring high precision and robustness, the head or high locations on the body are preferable. If the device must be worn on the torso, it is imperative to implement NLOS mitigation algorithms that can detect blocking conditions through indicators such as time-of-flight variance or signal power, and compensate for the bias before the filtering stage.

Finally, it is important to mention the limitations of the study, such as the use of a limited number of test subjects (three) and a simplified geometric scenario. Future research should address data collection with a larger number of subjects to generalize anthropometric effects and explore real-time adaptive bias estimation techniques to improve accuracy in critical locations such as the hip.
