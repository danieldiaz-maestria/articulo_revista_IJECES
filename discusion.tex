\subsection{Physical Determinants of Device Placement Reliability}

The experimental findings fundamentally challenge the "one-size-fits-all" approach to UWB tag placement. For the 6.5 GHz band, the severe impact of BS is not merely an attenuation factor (scaling noise), but a complex geometric dependency that structurally alters the underlying probability distribution of the ranging error. 

The superior performance of head placement under obstructed conditions (MAE $\approx$ 18.6 cm in NLOS compared to $\approx$ 97.7 cm for the hip) indicates that industrial safety and tracking applications should prioritize helmet-mounted or high-shoulder placements over belt-mounted alternatives, despite potential ergonomic trade-offs. This aligns with the findings of Otim \textit{et al.} \cite{ref4, Otim2019}, who identified the head (forehead) as the optimal placement, reporting minimal errors ($\approx$ 13 to 35 cm) due to a higher probability of LOS. The 6.5 GHz frequency presents a wavelength of approximately 4.6 cm, which enables a strong "creeping wave" diffraction effect around the curved geometry of the human skull, a propagation mechanism also explicitly recognized by Otim \textit{et al.} \cite{Otim2019} as key to allowing the signal to wrap around the physical obstruction efficiently without incurring catastrophic delay.

Conversely, the massive deterioration observed in torso placement (i.e., chest and hip) stems from complete physical occlusion. The torso functions as an insurmountable dielectric wall. When the torso blocks the LOS, the receiver relies entirely on weak multipath reflections that take significantly longer paths to reach the destination. This severe attenuation and the resulting positive bias (frequently adding delays of 0.2 to 0.5 m due to tissue transmission) confirm the observations reported by Tian \textit{et al.} \cite{ref16} and Otim \textit{et al.} \cite{ref4}. The nearly full-meter error under NLOS between the head and the hip provides empirical evidence that placing UWB trackers on a standard waist belt introduces unacceptable safety margins for precision applications.

\subsection{The "Constructive Multipath" Phenomenon}

A counterintuitive but critical discovery of this investigation is the "Constructive Multipath" effect observed in the indoor corridor scenario. Traditional RF theory posits that confined indoor environments inherently exhibit poorer positioning performance due to multi-path fading and interference, while open outdoor fields represent the ideal propagation scenario.

However, the empirical data reveal a paradox for highly mobile body placements. By performing a paired non-parametric comparison (i.e., Mann-Whitney U) between outdoor and indoor NLOS measurements for the same extremities, it was found that for the head, hand, wrist, knee, and ankle, the median ranging error was consistently and significantly \textit{lower} indoors than outdoors. For example, the hand placement exhibited a large effect size (i.e., $r = -0.693$, $p \ll 0.001$) favoring the confined corridor environment. 

The physical propagation mechanism driving this paradox is constructive multi-path. In a completely open outdoor field, when the human body blocks the direct LOS path, there are no environmental reflectors available to route the signal to the anchor. Consequently, the signal experiences severe attenuation, pushing the receiver to lock onto highly distorted ground reflections or to fail. However, in an indoor corridor, the walls, ceiling, and floor act as a dense reflective network. Welch \textit{et al.} \cite{ref15} noted that while the body can attenuate signals severely (i.e., up to 20-30 dB), indoor multipath is typically viewed as a source of degradation via "false first paths". Yet, the findings nuance this view: as suggested by Schmitt \textit{et al.} \cite{Schimtt2019} and Pradabphon \textit{et al.} \cite{Pradabphon2019}, architectural structures such as corridors channel energy and provide alternative paths that maintain connectivity. For extremities that constantly change spatial orientation (i.e., the hand or wrist), these diverse reflective paths "rescue" the signal, resulting in a significantly lower overall delay than the outdoor "pure obstruction" case. This confirms that indoor clutter can serve as a beneficial source of spatial diversity for 6.5 GHz UWB systems under BS constraints.

\subsection{Failure of Gaussian Assumptions in Parametric Models}

The most consequential theoretical finding of this study is the definitive statistical evidence that ranging errors generated by a 6.5 GHz BS do not follow a normal (Gaussian) distribution. As shown in Section \ref{sec:results}, the error distribution undergoes a structural transformation from a gamma distribution in LOS to a heavy-tailed log-normal distribution in NLOS.

This fundamentally explains the persistent failure of standard positioning algorithms, including Extended Kalman Filters (EKF) and Least Squares multilateration, to correct BS biases. Traditional state-estimation filters explicitly formulate their measurement update models assuming zero-mean Gaussian noise ($\mathcal{N}(0, R)$). However, the log-normal distribution of NLOS errors implies a massive positive skewness and a non-zero systematic bias. As argued by Miramá \textit{et al.} \cite{Mirama2021}, an EKF processing this heavy-tailed, non-zero mean noise subestimates the error and converges to a displaced trajectory. Similarly, Fan \cite{Fan2019} shows that least squares methods are invalidated by the outliers introduced during BS.

When a standard filter processes a log-normal noise profile, it interprets the systematic time delay introduced by the body as a legitimate physical displacement of the user. Consequently, the filter fails to correct the position and instead mathematically smooths the error, resulting in a trajectory that drifts smoothly away from reality. 

These findings establish an undeniable empirical baseline for the future design of UWB positioning systems: traditional Gaussian-based solvers are fundamentally flawed for human-centric tracking. Future systems require dynamic, "distribution-aware" architectures. Tanghe \textit{et al.} \cite{Tanghe2023} and Miramá \textit{et al.} \cite{ref20} have already pioneered the use of Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM) and particle filters to handle this multimodal nature. Building on that, the empirical characterization suggests that systems must be capable of detecting the onset of NLOS conditions and autonomously switching the measurement observation model from a Gaussian/gamma assumption to a heavy-tailed log-normal bias-compensation model in real time.
