During the last two decades, human interaction with the technological environment has undergone significant evolution, driven largely by the development of Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS). In this context, Ultra-Wideband (UWB) technology has emerged as a fundamental solution for its ability to provide sub-decimeter localization accuracy \cite{ref6, ref8}. Despite these theoretical advantages, its implementation in real-world scenarios faces critical obstacles, among which body obstruction or \textit{Body Shadowing} (BS) is one of the most complex and determining factors \cite{ref16}.

The BS phenomenon occurs when the human body interposes itself between the mobile device and the fixed reference nodes. Since biological tissue has high permittivity due to its water content, it attenuates, reflects, refracts, and diffracts radio-frequency signals \cite{ref15, ref18}. This effect is not negligible, as it can introduce ranging estimation errors that severely degrade system reliability in critical applications such as industrial safety \cite{Kianfar2020}, hospital monitoring, or sports performance analysis.

Previous studies have quantified the magnitude of this problem, highlighting its relevance for high-accuracy systems. Experimental investigations have reported that BS can introduce positioning errors ranging from 1.2 m to 4.5 m under severe NLOS (Non-Line-of-Sight) conditions \cite{ref4}. Other authors have documented signal attenuations of up to 15 dB \cite{Ruonan2019} and even extreme errors of up to 14 m at particularly unfavorable incidence angles \cite{Schimtt2019}. Likewise, comparisons of commercial systems have shown that although leading platforms like Qorvo (aka Decawave) can achieve 12 cm accuracy under ideal conditions, their performance degrades drastically without adequate mitigation algorithms, resulting in errors exceeding 1 m in complex environments \cite{Falcone2012}. These figures underscore that BS is not a marginal error in IPS but a fundamental barrier to technology adoption.

Although the scientific community has explored various mitigation strategies, most research has focused on the 3-5 GHz frequency range \cite{ref4, ref14}. The 6.5 GHz band remains relatively unexplored despite offering significant technical advantages, such as shorter wavelengths that improve temporal resolution. However, this higher-frequency band experiences greater attenuation in biological tissues, intensifying the challenges posed by BS. The core problem addressed in this study is that standard filtering models assume Gaussian error distributions. This hypothesis fails under the severe non-linear distortion introduced by the human body at 6.5 GHz.

The central objective of this article is to quantitatively characterize the impact of BS on 1D distance estimation for UWB systems operating at 6.5 GHz. This work represents a significant extension of preliminary research \cite{danny_daniel_and_victor_quintero}. In this expanded investigation, we contribute the following to the state of the art:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive Body Placement Analysis:} It systematically evaluates seven on-body tag placements (head, chest, hip, wrist, hand, ankle, knee) under consistent experimental conditions, thereby establishing a performance hierarchy to guide the design of future industrial safety systems.
    \item \textbf{Statistical Ranging Error Modeling for Human Tracking:} It shows through rigorous non-parametric testing and goodness-of-fit evaluations that body-induced ranging errors fundamentally follow a heavy-tailed Log-normal distribution, mathematically proving the failure of the Gaussian assumption typically used in standard tracking algorithms.
    \item \textbf{Spectral Characterization at 6.5 GHz and the Constructive Multipath Effect:} It provides specific empirical characterization for the 6.5 GHz band across indoor and outdoor scenarios, discovering the paradoxical "constructive multipath" effect where confined indoor environments partially compensate for severe body blockage on limb-mounted tags.
\end{itemize}

The rest of the article is structured as follows: Section \ref{sec:methodology} details the experimental methodology, including the hardware configuration, test scenarios, and data collection protocol. Section \ref{sec:results} presents the statistical characterization of the ranging error by the placement of the device on the body and channel condition. In Section \ref{sec:discussion}, the interpretation of the findings and their practical implications are discussed. Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the key conclusions and proposes directions for future research.
