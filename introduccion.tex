During the last two decades, human interaction with the technological environment has undergone significant evolution, driven largely by the development of Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS). In this context, Ultra-Wideband (UWB) technology has emerged as a fundamental solution for its ability to provide sub-decimeter localization accuracy \cite{ref6, ref8}. Despite these theoretical advantages, its implementation in real-world scenarios faces critical obstacles, among which \textit{body shadowing} (BS) is one of the most complex and determining factors \cite{ref16}.

The BS phenomenon occurs when the human body lies between the tag, i.e., the mobile node worn by the participant, and the anchor, i.e., the fixed reference node. Figure~\ref{fig:system_model} illustrates this condition by contrasting LOS and NLOS links between the tag and the anchor. Since biological tissue has high permittivity due to its water content, it attenuates, reflects, refracts, and diffracts radio-frequency signals \cite{ref15, ref18}. This effect is not negligible, as it can introduce ranging estimation errors that severely degrade system reliability in critical applications such as industrial safety \cite{Kianfar2020}, hospital monitoring, or sports performance analysis.

Previous studies have quantified the magnitude of this problem, highlighting its relevance for high-accuracy systems. Under LOS (Line-of-Sight) conditions, reported errors are generally lower and often in the decimetric range, depending on placement and experimental setup \cite{ref4, ref16, ref18}. However, under NLOS (Non-Line-of-Sight) conditions, the degradation is substantial: errors from 1.2 m to 4.5 m have been reported \cite{ref4}, extreme errors up to 14 m have been documented at unfavorable incidence angles \cite{Schimtt2019}, and additional studies report attenuations up to 15 dB caused by BS \cite{Ruonan2019}. These figures underscore that BS is not a marginal error in IPS but a fundamental barrier to technology adoption.\\

Although the scientific community has explored various mitigation strategies, most research has focused on the 3-5 GHz frequency range \cite{ref4, ref14}. The 6.5 GHz band remains relatively unexplored and is still relevant for UWB experimentation. In ToF-based ranging, temporal resolution is primarily determined by pulse duration and receiver timing capabilities rather than by wavelength alone; shorter pulses, e.g., picosecond-scale pulses, can enable finer $\Delta t$ estimation, subject to practical electronic constraints. However, operation at this band also experiences greater attenuation in biological tissues, intensifying the challenges posed by BS. The central focus of this study is the estimation of ranging error under BS and its statistical association with probability distributions, particularly under NLOS, rather than the design of filtering algorithms.\\

The central objective of this article is to quantitatively characterize the impact of BS on 1D distance estimation for UWB systems operating at 6.5 GHz. This work represents a significant extension of preliminary research \cite{danny_daniel_and_victor_quintero}. In this expanded investigation, the following contributions are made to the state of the art:\\

\textbf{Comprehensive Body Placement Analysis:} It systematically evaluates seven on-body tag placements (head, chest, hip, wrist, hand, ankle, knee) under consistent experimental conditions, thereby establishing a performance hierarchy to guide tracking and localization functions in future industrial safety systems.\\

\textbf{Statistical Ranging Error Modeling for Human Tracking:} It shows through rigorous non-parametric testing and goodness-of-fit evaluations that body-induced ranging errors fundamentally follow a heavy-tailed Log-normal distribution, mathematically proving the failure of the Gaussian assumption typically used in standard tracking algorithms.\\

\textbf{Spectral Characterization at 6.5 GHz and the Constructive Multipath Effect:} It provides specific empirical characterization for the 6.5 GHz band across indoor and outdoor scenarios, documenting the paradoxical "constructive multipath" effect where confined indoor environments partially compensate for severe BS on limb-mounted tags.


The rest of the article is structured as follows: Section \ref{sec:methodology} details the experimental methodology, including the hardware configuration, experimental scenarios, data-collection protocol, and statistical-analysis procedure. Section \ref{sec:results} presents the statistical characterization of the ranging error by tag placement and channel condition. In Section \ref{sec:discussion}, the interpretation of the findings and their practical implications are discussed. Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the key conclusions and proposes directions for future research.
