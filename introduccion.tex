During the last two decades, human interaction with the technological environment has undergone significant evolution, driven largely by the development of Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS). In this context, Ultra-Wideband (UWB) technology has stood out as a fundamental solution due to its ability to provide sub-decimeter localization accuracy \cite{ref6, ref8}. Despite these theoretical advantages, its implementation in real-world scenarios faces critical obstacles, among which body obstruction or \textit{Body Shadowing} (BS) is one of the most complex and determining factors \cite{ref16}.

The BS phenomenon occurs when the human body interposes itself between the mobile device and the fixed reference nodes. Since biological tissue possesses high permittivity due to its water content, it acts as a medium that attenuates, refracts, and diffracts radio frequency signals \cite{ref15, ref18}. This effect is not negligible, as it can introduce distance estimation errors that severely degrade system reliability in critical applications such as industrial safety \cite{Kianfar2020}, hospital monitoring, or sports performance analysis.

Previous studies have quantified the magnitude of this problem, highlighting its relevance for high-precision systems. Experimental investigations have reported that body obstruction can introduce positioning errors ranging from 1.2 m to 4.5 m under severe NLOS (Non-Line-of-Sight) conditions \cite{ref4}. Other authors have documented signal attenuations of up to 15 dB \cite{Ruonan2019} and even extreme errors of up to 14 m at particularly unfavorable incidence angles \cite{Schimtt2019}. Likewise, comparisons of commercial systems have shown that, although leading platforms like Decawave (now Qorvo) can achieve an accuracy error of 12 cm under ideal conditions, their performance degrades drastically without adequate mitigation algorithms, recording errors exceeding 1 m in complex environments \cite{Falcone2012}. These figures underscore that BS is not a marginal error, but a fundamental barrier to technology adoption.

Although the scientific community has explored various mitigation strategies, most research has focused on the 3 to 5 GHz frequency range \cite{ref4, ref14}. The 6.5 GHz band, in contrast, remains relatively unexplored despite offering significant technical advantages, such as shorter wavelengths that improve temporal resolution and, potentially, distance estimation precision. However, this higher frequency band experiences more pronounced attenuation in biological tissues, intensifying the challenges imposed by BS. This gap in the literature represents a critical opportunity to systematically characterize BS behavior in a little-studied frequency range, establishing statistical models that can inform more sophisticated filtering algorithms.

The central objective of this article is to quantitatively characterize the impact of BS on UWB systems operating at 6.5 GHz using a two-phase methodology. This work represents a significant extension of preliminary research \cite{danny_daniel_and_victor_quintero}, where an initial BS analysis was performed at seven body locations. In this expanded investigation, a structured experimental protocol was implemented comprising: (\textit{i}) a point-to-point distance characterization phase evaluating all body locations under LOS/NLOS conditions, and (\textit{ii}) a 2D positioning validation phase in an experimental hall with four fixed anchors. Crucially, the statistical characterization from the first phase—specifically, the demonstration that NLOS errors exhibit Log-normal distributions instead of Gaussian—was used to adjust the parameters of the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) used in the second phase. This integrated approach allows not only quantifying the precision degradation caused by BS but also validating that robust filtering algorithms, calibrated with real statistical models, can partially mitigate these effects in dynamic localization scenarios.

The article is structured as follows: Section \ref{sec:methodology} details the experimental methodology employed, including hardware configuration, test scenarios, and data collection protocol. Section \ref{sec:results} presents the results obtained, analyzing distance error by body location and the implications for 2D positioning. In Section \ref{sec:discussion}, the main findings, their practical implications, and the limitations of the study are discussed. Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the key conclusions and proposes directions for future research in this field.
