During the last two decades, human interaction with the technological environment has undergone significant evolution, driven largely by the development of Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS). In this context, Ultra-Wideband (UWB) technology has stood out as a fundamental solution due to its ability to provide sub-decimeter localization accuracy \cite{ref6, ref8}. Despite these theoretical advantages, its implementation in real-world scenarios faces critical obstacles, among which body obstruction or \textit{Body Shadowing} (BS) is one of the most complex and determining factors \cite{ref16}.

The BS phenomenon occurs when the human body interposes itself between the mobile device and the fixed reference nodes. Since biological tissue has high permittivity due to its water content, it attenuates, refracts, and diffracts radio-frequency signals \cite{ref15, ref18}. This effect is not negligible, as it can introduce distance estimation errors that severely degrade system reliability in critical applications such as industrial safety \cite{Kianfar2020}, hospital monitoring, or sports performance analysis.

Previous studies have quantified the magnitude of this problem, highlighting its relevance for high-precision systems. Experimental investigations have reported that body obstruction can introduce positioning errors ranging from 1.2 m to 4.5 m under severe NLOS (Non-Line-of-Sight) conditions \cite{ref4}. Other authors have documented signal attenuations of up to 15 dB \cite{Ruonan2019} and even extreme errors of up to 14 m at particularly unfavorable incidence angles \cite{Schimtt2019}. Likewise, comparisons of commercial systems have shown that although leading platforms like Decawave (now Qorvo) can achieve 12 cm accuracy under ideal conditions, their performance degrades drastically without adequate mitigation algorithms, resulting in errors exceeding 1 m in complex environments \cite{Falcone2012}. These figures underscore that BS is not a marginal error, but a fundamental barrier to technology adoption.

Although the scientific community has explored various mitigation strategies, most research has focused on the 3 to 5 GHz frequency range \cite{ref4, ref14}. The 6.5 GHz band (Channel 5) remains relatively unexplored despite offering significant technical advantages, such as shorter wavelengths that improve temporal resolution. However, this higher-frequency band experiences greater attenuation in biological tissues, intensifying the challenges posed by BS. The core problem addressed in this study is that standard positioning algorithms assume Gaussian error distributions, a hypothesis that fails under the severe non-linear distortion introduced by the human body at 6.5 GHz, resulting in unreliable tracking.

The central objective of this article is to quantitatively characterize the impact of BS on UWB systems operating at 6.5 GHz using a two-phase methodology. This work represents a significant extension of preliminary research \cite{danny_daniel_and_victor_quintero}. In this expanded investigation, we contribute the following to the state of the art:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Spectral Characterization at 6.5 GHz:} Unlike the extensive literature on the 3.5-4.5 GHz bands, we provide a specific empirical characterization for the 6.5 GHz band, identifying frequency-dependent propagation behaviors such as the constructive multipath effect in indoor corridors.
    \item \textbf{Statistical Error Modeling for Human Tracking:} We demonstrate through goodness-of-fit tests that body-induced ranging errors follow a heavy-tailed Log-normal distribution, challenging the Gaussian assumption typically used in standard tracking algorithms.
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive Body Placement Analysis:} We systematically evaluate seven on-body tag locations under consistent experimental conditions, establishing a performance hierarchy that guides the design of future industrial safety systems.
    \item \textbf{Implementation and Validation of a Real-Time Positioning System:} We designed and implemented a complete 2D localization system, performing a comparative analysis between Least Squares Multilateration and an Extended Kalman Filter (EKF). This validation demonstrates that EKF, when tuned with empirical noise statistics, offers superior stability in dynamic tracking scenarios compared to static solvers.
\end{itemize}

The rest of the article is structured as follows: Section \ref{sec:methodology} details the experimental methodology employed, including hardware configuration, test scenarios, and data collection protocol. Section \ref{sec:results} presents the results obtained, analyzing distance error by body location and the implications for 2D positioning. In Section \ref{sec:discussion}, the main findings, their practical implications, and the limitations of the study are discussed. Finally, Section \ref{sec:conclusions} summarizes the key conclusions and proposes directions for future research in this field.
