\textbf{Abstract - }\textit{Ultra-Wideband (UWB) technology has emerged as a promising solution for Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS) due to its high precision. However, its performance in human tracking applications is significantly affected by the body shadowing (BS) phenomenon, which degrades localization accuracy. This article presents a rigorous experimental characterization of BS effects on UWB IPS operating in the 6.5 GHz band, a frequency substantially less explored than the conventional 3--5 GHz range. We evaluated seven body-worn locations (head, chest, hip, wrist, hand, ankle, knee) using four fixed anchors and collected Time of Flight (ToF) measurements under Line of Sight (LOS) and Non-Line of Sight (NLOS) conditions in indoor and outdoor scenarios. Our findings demonstrate that device location critically determines positioning performance, with head-mounted placement yielding the most robust results (MAE of 4.87 cm in outdoor LOS, degrading to 18.16 cm in indoor NLOS), while hip-mounted placement exhibits severe degradation (7.22 cm to 95.11 cm across LOS/NLOS conditions). Statistical analysis reveals that NLOS errors follow a Log-normal distribution with heavy tails, significantly departing from Gaussian assumptions and introducing systematic bias in distance measurements. While non-linear filtering (Extended Kalman Filter) effectively reduces temporal noise, standard algorithms cannot correct such bias without external references, necessitating bias-aware or outlier-rejection strategies beyond conventional non-linear filtering. We employed an EKF informed by phase-one statistical characterization to achieve 2D positioning accuracy in the range of 55--95 cm depending on body location, achieving robust performance for head placement (18.16 cm) but demonstrating fundamental limitations for severe shadowing (hip: 95.11 cm), validating UWB viability for practical room-level localization applications with appropriate body placement constraints.}
