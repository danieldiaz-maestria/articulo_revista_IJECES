    \textbf{Abstract - }	\textit{Ultra-Wideband (UWB) technology has emerged as a promising solution for Indoor Positioning Systems (IPS) due to its high accuracy. However, its performance in human tracking applications is significantly affected by body shadowing (BS), which degrades localization accuracy. This article presents an experimental characterization of BS effects on a UWB ranging system at 6.5 GHz, a frequency band substantially less explored than the conventional 3-5 GHz range. We evaluated seven body-worn placements (head, chest, hip, wrist, hand, ankle, knee) using a fixed node. We collected Time-of-Flight (ToF) measurements under Line-of-Sight (LOS) and Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS) conditions in indoor and outdoor scenarios, recording over 100,000 independent range samples. The findings show that tag placement on the body critically determines distance estimation performance, with head-mounted placement yielding the most robust results (MAE of 4.87 cm in outdoor LOS, degrading to 18.16 cm in indoor NLOS). In addition, LOS results in indoor conditions show clear differences across placements (i.e., MAE of 10.43 cm at the head, 13.74 cm at the chest, and 7.19 cm at the hip), while torso placements exhibit severe degradation under NLOS (i.e., errors of up to 95.11 cm). Furthermore, our statistical analysis indicates that NLOS ranging errors are better approximated by a heavy-tailed Log-normal distribution, deviating from Gaussian-error approximations commonly assumed in probabilistic ranging models. It is also documented that an unexpected  constructive multipath effect occurs in confined indoor spaces, where environmental reflections partially compensate for direct-path obstruction on limb-mounted tags, paradoxically yielding lower NLOS ranging estimation errors indoor than outdoor scenarios. These findings provide an empirical baseline for future robust, Log-normal-aware algorithm design for UWB body shadowing mitigation.}
