The research was based on a comprehensive Data-Driven Methodology designed to systematically characterize the statistical models of the ranging error induced by BS.

\textbf{Hardware Configuration.} The experiments used Qorvo DWM1001 development modules, configured to operate at a center frequency of 6.5 GHz (Channel 5) \cite{ref8}. The experimental system consisted of one fixed reference node (anchor) and a mobile node (tag) carried by human participants.

\textbf{Experimental Scenarios.} The characterization was executed in two distinct environments, deliberately chosen to isolate different aspects of the physical propagation:
\textit{Scenario 1 (Outdoor Open Field):} Static measurements were performed in an open outdoor field (approximate dimensions of 20~m $\times$ 30~m) selected to minimize environmental reflections and isolate the direct impact of BS.
\textit{Scenario 2 (Indoor Corridor):} A second set of measurements was performed in an indoor corridor (approximately 2~m wide, 50~m long, with nearby walls, floor, and ceilings) to introduce multipath effects representative of typical indoor navigation applications. This duality of scenarios allowed differentiation between attenuation due to body obstruction (outdoor) and composite attenuation with reflections (indoor).

\textbf{The distance characterization} protocol, for which seven body placements for the mobile node were evaluated to represent typical wearable use-cases: head, chest, hip, hand, wrist, knee, and ankle (Figure~\ref{fig:posiciones}). For each body placement of the mobile node, measurements were collected under two propagation conditions: Line-of-Sight (LOS), with a direct path between the anchor and the mobile node; and Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS), where the signal was deliberately obstructed by rotating the participant's body $180^{\circ}$ to ensure complete occlusion of the mobile node from the anchor by the torso.

Measurements were performed at 13 static positions, spaced in 1-meter increments at distances from 1~m to 13~m from the reference anchor. At each position, 250 independent distance measurements were recorded, accumulating over 100,000 measurements in total (13 positions $\times$ 7 body placements $\times$ 2 channel conditions $\times$ 250 samples $\times$ 2 scenarios). This massive volume of empirical data enabled the construction of highly robust statistical models to characterize the distribution of ranging errors under each condition.

\textbf{Statistical Analysis Protocol.} The ranging error was calculated as ${e_i = |d_{\text{measured},i} - d_{\text{real},i}|}$ for each measurement. To rigorously evaluate the statistical nature of the error, theoretical probability distributions (gamma, log-normal, exponential, normal) were fitted to the empirical data using Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) were employed to quantitatively select the most accurate underlying distribution model. Furthermore, non-parametric inferential tests, specifically the Shapiro-Wilk test for normality and the Mann-Whitney U test, were applied to assess the statistical significance of differences observed across body placements and conditions.
