The research was based on a comprehensive Data-Driven Methodology designed to systematically characterize the statistical models of the ranging error induced by BS.

\textbf{Hardware Configuration.} The experiments used Qorvo DWM1001 development modules, configured to operate at a center frequency of 6.5 GHz (Channel 5) \cite{ref8}. The experimental system consisted of one anchor and one tag carried by human participants.

\textbf{System Model Description.} As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:system_model}, the anchor was installed at a known reference position, while the tag was placed on different parts of the participant's body. The ranging process was based on ToF measurements between the tag and the anchor under LOS and NLOS conditions. This configuration captures the direct impact of BS on ranging error while maintaining a controlled, reproducible setup.

\textbf{Experimental Scenarios.} The characterization was executed in two distinct scenarios, deliberately chosen to isolate different aspects of the physical propagation:

\textit{Scenario 1 (Outdoor Open Field):} Static measurements were performed in an open outdoor field (approximate dimensions of 20~m $\times$ 30~m) selected to minimize environmental reflections and isolate the direct impact of BS on ToF-based ranging error.

\textit{Scenario 2 (Indoor Corridor):} A second set of measurements was performed in an indoor corridor (approximately 2~m wide, 50~m long, with nearby walls, floor, and ceilings) to introduce multipath effects representative of typical indoor navigation applications. This duality of scenarios allowed differentiation between a reduced-multipath baseline (outdoor) and a reflection-rich condition (indoor), where reflected, diffracted, and refracted components interact as signal echoes. These interactions affect attenuation and first-path detection, directly impacting ToF estimation and, consequently, the ranging error.

\textbf{The ranging characterization} protocol evaluated seven body placements for the tag to represent typical wearable use-cases: head, chest, hip, hand, wrist, knee, and ankle, as shown in Figure~\ref{fig:system_model} and Figure~\ref{fig:posiciones}. For each considered body part, range estimation was performed relative to a single anchor using the Time of Flight (ToF) signal metric between the tag and the anchor. Measurements were collected under two propagation conditions: Line-of-Sight (LOS), with a direct path between the tag and the anchor; and Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS), where BS was deliberately induced by rotating the participant's body $180^{\circ}$ so the participant's body lay between the tag and the anchor.

Although attenuation was not used as the primary ranging metric, it was considered a relevant propagation factor because severe NLOS attenuation, particularly in outdoor conditions, can limit link reliability between the tag and anchor and affect the ToF estimation quality.

Measurements were performed at 13 static positions, spaced in 1~m increments at distances from 1~m to 13~m from the anchor. At each position, 250 independent distance measurements were recorded for each channel condition, i.e., LOS and NLOS, for each body placement, and for each scenario, accumulating over 100,000 measurements in total (13 positions $\times$ 7 body placements $\times$ 2 channel conditions $\times$ 250 samples $\times$ 2 scenarios). This massive volume of empirical data enabled the construction of highly robust statistical models to characterize the distribution of ranging errors under each condition.

\textbf{Statistical Analysis Protocol.} The ranging error was calculated as ${e_i = |d_{\text{measured},i} - d_{\text{real},i}|}$ for each measurement. Since this absolute-error metric is non-negative by definition, the primary fitted families were positive-support distributions (gamma, log-normal, and exponential), estimated via Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) were employed to quantitatively select the most accurate underlying distribution model. Normality was assessed only as a reference hypothesis, commonly assumed in Gaussian-noise models (using the Shapiro-Wilk test and Q-Q plots), not as the expected final model for absolute error. Alternative candidates, such as Weibull or Rayleigh, can also model positive errors and are relevant for extended analyses; Student's t is more appropriate for symmetric signed residuals and was therefore not prioritized in this absolute-error formulation. Furthermore, non-parametric inferential tests, specifically the Mann-Whitney U test, were applied to assess the statistical significance of differences observed across body placements and conditions.
