The research was based on a comprehensive Data-Driven Methodology designed to systematically characterize the statistical models of the ranging error induced by BS.

\textbf{Hardware Configuration.} The experiments used Qorvo DWM1001 development modules, configured to operate at a center frequency of 6.5 GHz (Channel 5) \cite{ref8}. The experimental system consisted of one fixed node and a mobile node (tag) carried by human participants.

\textbf{System Model Description.} Figure~\ref{fig:system_model} presents the experimental model used in this study. The fixed node was installed at a known reference position, while the mobile node (tag) was placed on different parts of the participant's body. The ranging process was based on ToF measurements between both nodes under LOS and NLOS conditions. This configuration captures the direct impact of body shadowing on ranging error while preserving a controlled and reproducible setup.

\textbf{Experimental Scenarios.} The characterization was executed in two distinct environments, deliberately chosen to isolate different aspects of the physical propagation:
\textit{Scenario 1 (Outdoor Open Field):} Static measurements were performed in an open outdoor field (approximate dimensions of 20~m $\times$ 30~m) selected to minimize environmental reflections and isolate the direct impact of body obstruction on ToF-based ranging error.
\textit{Scenario 2 (Indoor Corridor):} A second set of measurements was performed in an indoor corridor (approximately 2~m wide, 50~m long, with nearby walls, floor, and ceilings) to introduce multipath effects representative of typical indoor navigation applications. This duality of scenarios allowed differentiation between a reduced-multipath baseline (outdoor) and a reflection-rich condition (indoor), where reflected, diffracted, and refracted components interact as signal echoes. These interactions affect attenuation and first-path detection, directly impacting ToF estimation and, consequently, ranging error.

\textbf{The ranging distance characterization} protocol evaluated seven body placements for the mobile node to represent typical wearable use-cases: head, chest, hip, hand, wrist, knee, and ankle (Figure~\ref{fig:posiciones}). For each considered body part, distance estimation was performed against a single fixed reference node using the Time of Flight (ToF) signal metric between the fixed node and the mobile node. Measurements were collected under two propagation conditions: Line-of-Sight (LOS), with a direct path between the fixed node and the mobile node; and Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS), where the signal was deliberately obstructed by rotating the participant's body $180^{\circ}$ to ensure complete occlusion of the mobile node from the fixed node by the torso.

Although attenuation was not used as the primary ranging metric, it was considered a relevant propagation factor because severe NLOS attenuation, particularly in outdoor conditions, can limit link reliability between nodes and affect ToF estimation quality.

Measurements were performed at 13 static positions, spaced in 1-meter increments at distances from 1~m to 13~m from the fixed node. At each position, 250 independent distance measurements were recorded for each channel condition (LOS and NLOS), for each body placement, and for each scenario, accumulating over 100,000 measurements in total (13 positions $\times$ 7 body placements $\times$ 2 channel conditions $\times$ 250 samples $\times$ 2 scenarios). This massive volume of empirical data enabled the construction of highly robust statistical models to characterize the distribution of ranging errors under each condition.

\textbf{Statistical Analysis Protocol.} The ranging error was calculated as ${e_i = |d_{\text{measured},i} - d_{\text{real},i}|}$ for each measurement. Since this absolute-error metric is non-negative by definition, the primary fitted families were positive-support distributions (gamma, log-normal, and exponential), estimated via Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). The Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) were employed to quantitatively select the most accurate underlying distribution model. Normality was assessed only as a reference hypothesis commonly assumed by Gaussian-noise models (using Shapiro-Wilk and Q-Q analysis), not as the expected final model for absolute error. Alternative candidates such as Weibull or Rayleigh can also model positive errors and are relevant for extended analyses; Student's t is more appropriate for symmetric signed residuals and was therefore not prioritized for this absolute-error formulation. Furthermore, non-parametric inferential tests, specifically the Mann-Whitney U test, were applied to assess the statistical significance of differences observed across body placements and conditions.
