The experimental evaluation provides crucial empirical evidence regarding the ranging error behavior of UWB systems operating in the 6.5 GHz band. This phase focuses on the systematic quantification of ranging errors across seven body placements, establishing a baseline for tag placement and showing the BS-induced statistical shift in error characteristics.

\subsection{Quantification of BS Impact by Body Placement}

To determine the most robust tag placement, a comparative analysis of the 1D ranging error was performed in the order recommended for interpretation: first, the indoor corridor is examined by contrasting LOS and NLOS conditions; then, the outdoor open-field scenario is evaluated using the same LOS/NLOS sequence. The reported metrics are Mean Absolute Error (MAE), Standard Deviation ($\sigma$), and Maximum Error; therefore, the tables report mean-based statistics and do not report median or mode.

Table \ref{tab:resumen_indoor} summarizes the performance metrics for the Indoor Corridor scenario, where multipath effects are strongly present. In indoor LOS, the lowest MAE is obtained at the wrist (6.42 cm), followed by the hip (7.19 cm), whereas the ankle shows the maximum LOS MAE (41.50 cm). In indoor NLOS, the head provides the best body-placement performance (18.66 cm), while the hip is the worst case (97.76 cm). This LOS-to-NLOS reading shows that the indoor corridor can preserve usable ranging for head and limb placements, but torso-adjacent placements remain critically affected by BS.

After the outdoor LOS baseline is established, the outdoor NLOS data confirms the placement hierarchy observed indoors while changing the best-performing body location. The wrist placement demonstrates superior outdoor NLOS performance, with MAE = 24.07 cm, followed by the head (MAE = 32.05 cm) and the ankle (MAE = 38.86 cm). Torso placements remain critically degraded: the hip is the worst outdoor NLOS case, with MAE = 94.78 cm, while the chest reaches MAE = 79.00 cm. The tag baseline without body shadowing, reported separately in the source measurements, achieves MAE = 9.99 cm and $\sigma_{AE}$ = 4.46 cm, establishing the reference performance without body-induced BS.

When indoor and outdoor results are compared, the most important pattern is that NLOS degradation depends jointly on body placement and available multipath. Indoor NLOS favors the head (18.66 cm), whereas outdoor NLOS favors the wrist (24.07 cm). The worst case is consistently associated with the hip under NLOS, reaching 97.76 cm indoors and 94.78 cm outdoors. Torso placements, i.e., chest and hip, exhibit critical degradation, with NLOS ranging error increasing by a factor of over 13$\times$ compared to LOS conditions, i.e., the hip transitions from 7.19 cm in indoor LOS to 97.76 cm in indoor NLOS. The recorded Maximum Error sample at the hip, i.e., 2.9 m, indicates that placing the tag on a belt clip effectively renders standard ranging algorithms unsuitable for precision tracking under BS.

\subsection{Probabilistic Modeling of Body Shadowing Ranging Error}

Statistical error modeling is essential for designing robust navigation algorithms. Because the analyzed metric is absolute ranging error ($e_i \geq 0$), the primary candidate families were positive-support distributions (gamma, log-normal, and exponential). Traditional filters, such as the standard Kalman Filter, explicitly assume Gaussian (normal) measurement noise; therefore, normality was evaluated only as a reference hypothesis, not as the expected final model for absolute error. The fit of these distributions was evaluated using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) on the entire dataset of 100,000+ samples.

The distribution analysis in Table \ref{tab:aic_bic} is reported at the global level (all body placements combined) to identify the overall BS-induced statistical shift between LOS and NLOS. In parallel, placement-dependent behavior is explicitly analyzed through Table \ref{tab:resumen_indoor} and subsequent non-parametric contrasts. This combined view is consistent with prior body-shadowing studies that report both global channel-condition behavior and body-placement-specific effects \cite{ref4, ref14, Otim2019}.

\subsection{Non-Parametric Statistical Testing}
Because the data severely violate the assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity, i.e., Levene's test, $p \ll 0.001$, standard parametric tests such as ANOVA are methodologically invalid for this dataset. Consequently, the Mann-Whitney U test was utilized to quantify the magnitude of the differences between locations. The contrast between the best-performing placements (Head/Wrist) and the worst-performing placements (Hip/Chest) yielded an exceptionally large effect size, i.e., $r = 0.968$ in NLOS, $p \ll 0.001$, showing conclusively that tag placement on the body is a dominant, statistically undeniable factor in determining the reliability of 6.5 GHz UWB systems.
