The experimental evaluation provides crucial empirical evidence regarding the performance of ranging error in UWB systems operating in the 6.5 GHz band. This phase focuses on the systematic quantification of ranging errors across seven body placements, establishing a baseline for node placement and showing the fundamental statistical shift in error characteristics induced by BS.

The comparative analysis establishes a clear hierarchy of robustness for tag placement. The head placement emerges as optimal, maintaining stability with modest degradation (i.e., MAE $\approx$ 18.6 cm in NLOS). This performance is attributed to the creeping-wave diffraction phenomenon around the curvature of the human skull, allowing 6.5 GHz UWB signals to bend and maintain communication even when visually obstructed.

Conversely, torso placements (i.e., chest and hip) exhibit critical degradation, with NLOS ranging error increasing by a factor of over 13$\times$ compared to LOS conditions (i.e., Hip transitioning from 7.19 cm in LOS to 97.76 cm in NLOS). The human torso acts as an insurmountable electromagnetic wall at 6.5 GHz, effectively blocking the direct path. The massive Maximum Error recorded at the hip (i.e., 2.9 m) highlights that placing the tag on a belt clip effectively renders standard ranging algorithms useless for precision tracking.

This degradation is visually striking when examining the error distribution across channel conditions. \subsection{Probabilistic Modeling of Body Shadowing Ranging Error}

Traditional filters, such as the standard Kalman Filter, explicitly assume that measurement noise follows a Gaussian (normal) distribution. For NLOS conditions, the statistical structure of the error undergoes a radical transformation. Table \ref{tab:aic_bic} and Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_nlos} show that the log-normal distribution offers the best fit, displacing the gamma and exponential distribution models. This transition from gamma to log-normal reflects the multiplicative nature of noise in strongly obstructed environments, where ranging error can be modeled as the product of attenuation and reflection factors rather than as simple additive Gaussian noise.

The profound deviation from normality under BS is mathematically critical. Figure \ref{fig:qq_nlos} presents the Quantile-Quantile (Q-Q) plot for the NLOS data against a theoretical Normal distribution. The extreme curvature and divergence in the upper tail confirm that large positive errors (biases) are vastly more frequent than a Gaussian model would predict. The Shapiro-Wilk normality test yielded $p \ll 0.001$, firmly rejecting the Gaussian hypothesis.

\subsection{Non-Parametric Statistical Testing}

Because the data severely violate the assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity (i.e., Levene's test, $p \ll 0.001$), standard parametric tests such as ANOVA are methodologically invalid for this dataset. Consequently, it was utilized the Mann-Whitney U test to quantify the magnitude of the differences between locations. The contrast between the best-performing placements (Head/Wrist) and the worst-performing placements (Hip/Chest) yielded an exceptionally large effect size (i.e., $r = 0.968$ in NLOS, $p \ll 0.001$), showing conclusively that node placement on the body is a dominant, statistically undeniable factor in determining the reliability of 6.5 GHz UWB systems.
