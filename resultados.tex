The experimental evaluation provides crucial empirical evidence regarding the performance of UWB systems operating in the 6.5 GHz band. This phase focuses on the systematic quantification of ranging errors across seven body locations, establishing a baseline for sensor placement and proving the fundamental statistical shift in error characteristics induced by Body Shadowing.

The comparative analysis establishes a clear hierarchy of robustness for wearable tag placement. The head location emerges as the optimal placement, maintaining stability with a modest degradation (MAE $\approx$ 18.6 cm in NLOS). This performance is attributed to the creeping wave diffraction phenomenon around the curvature of the human skull, allowing the 6.5 GHz UWB signals to bend and maintain communication even when visually obstructed.

Conversely, torso locations (chest and hip) exhibit critical degradation, with NLOS errors increasing by a factor of over 13$\times$ compared to LOS conditions (e.g., Hip transitioning from 7.19 cm in LOS to 97.76 cm in NLOS). The human torso acts as an insurmountable electromagnetic wall at 6.5 GHz, effectively blocking the direct path. The massive Maximum Error recorded at the hip (2.9 meters) highlights that placing the tag on a belt clip effectively renders standard ranging algorithms useless for precision tracking.

This degradation is visually striking when examining the error distribution across channel conditions. \subsection{Probabilistic Modeling of Body Shadowing Error}

Statistical error modeling is essential for designing robust navigation algorithms. Traditional filters, such as the standard Kalman Filter, explicitly assume that measurement noise follows a Gaussian (Normal) distribution. To validate this assumption, we evaluated the fit of various theoretical distributions (Gamma, Log-normal, Exponential) using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) on the entire dataset of 100,000+ samples.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:aic_bic}, in LOS conditions, the Gamma distribution proves to be the optimal model (Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_los}), effectively capturing the slight positive skewness generated by hardware noise and minor multi-path reflections. However, in NLOS conditions, the statistical structure of the error undergoes a radical transformation. 

Table \ref{tab:aic_bic} and Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_nlos} conclusively demonstrate that the \textbf{Log-normal distribution} offers the best fit for NLOS data, displacing the Gamma and Exponential models. The transition from Gamma to Log-normal reflects the multiplicative nature of the noise in strongly obstructed environments, where the distance error can be approximately expressed as a product of multiple attenuation and reflection factors, rather than simple additive Gaussian noise.

The profound deviation from normality under Body Shadowing is mathematically critical. Figure \ref{fig:qq_nlos} presents the Quantile-Quantile (Q-Q) plot for the NLOS data against a theoretical Normal distribution. The extreme curvature and divergence in the upper tail confirm that large positive errors (bias) are vastly more frequent than a Gaussian model would predict. The Shapiro-Wilk normality test yielded $p \ll 0.001$, firmly rejecting the Gaussian hypothesis.

\subsection{Non-Parametric Statistical Testing}

Because the data severely violates normality and homoscedasticity assumptions (Levene's test, $p \ll 0.001$), standard parametric tests like ANOVA are methodologically invalid for this dataset. Consequently, we utilized the Mann-Whitney U test to quantify the magnitude of the differences between locations. The contrast between the best-performing locations (Head/Wrist) and the worst-performing locations (Hip/Chest) yielded an exceptionally large effect size ($r = 0.968$ in NLOS, $p \ll 0.001$), demonstrating conclusively that sensor placement on the body is a dominant, statistically undeniable factor in determining the reliability of 6.5 GHz UWB systems.
