The experimental evaluation provides crucial empirical evidence regarding the ranging error behavior of UWB systems operating in the 6.5 GHz band. This phase focuses on the systematic quantification of ranging errors across seven body placements, establishing a baseline for node placement and showing the BS-induced statistical shift in error characteristics.

The comparative analysis establishes a clear hierarchy of robustness for tag placement. The head placement emerges as optimal, maintaining stability with modest degradation (i.e., MAE $\approx$ 18.6 cm in NLOS). This performance is attributed to the creeping-wave diffraction phenomenon around the curvature of the human skull, allowing 6.5 GHz UWB signals to bend and maintain communication even when visually obstructed.

Outdoor results were also analyzed and are consistent with the same placement-dependent trend. In open-field LOS, reduced environmental reflections lead to lower ToF-derived ranging bias for favorable placements (e.g., head placement reaches MAE values around 4.87 cm), while under outdoor NLOS the lack of helpful reflectors increases the impact of body blockage and raises ranging bias, especially for torso and limb placements.

Conversely, torso placements (i.e., chest and hip) exhibit critical degradation, with NLOS ranging error increasing by a factor of over 13$\times$ compared to LOS conditions (i.e., Hip transitioning from 7.19 cm in LOS to 97.76 cm in NLOS). The human torso acts as an insurmountable electromagnetic wall at 6.5 GHz, effectively blocking the direct path. The massive Maximum Error recorded at the hip (i.e., 2.9 m) highlights that placing the tag on a belt clip effectively renders standard ranging algorithms useless for precision tracking.

A higher noise level is observed in indoor data, but this environment also exhibits a constructive multipath effect that preserves observable measurements in both LOS and NLOS conditions along the evaluated range. In contrast, outdoor measurements present lower visible noise, but after the 9 m mark the distributions become strongly degraded and no clear pattern is appreciable.

\subsection{Probabilistic Modeling of Body Shadowing Ranging Error}

Statistical error modeling is essential for designing robust navigation algorithms. Because the analyzed metric is absolute ranging error ($e_i \geq 0$), the primary candidate families were positive-support distributions (gamma, log-normal, and exponential). Traditional filters, such as the standard Kalman Filter, explicitly assume Gaussian (normal) measurement noise; therefore, normality was evaluated only as a reference hypothesis, not as the expected final model for absolute error. The fit of these distributions was evaluated using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) and Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) on the entire dataset of 100,000+ samples.

The distribution analysis in Table \ref{tab:aic_bic} is reported at global level (all body placements combined) to identify the overall BS-induced statistical shift between LOS and NLOS. In parallel, placement-dependent behavior is explicitly analyzed through Table \ref{tab:resumen_indoor} and subsequent non-parametric contrasts. This combined view is consistent with prior body-shadowing studies that report both global channel-condition behavior and body-placement-specific effects \cite{ref4, ref14, Otim2019}.

For NLOS conditions, the statistical structure of the error undergoes a radical transformation. Table \ref{tab:aic_bic} and Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_nlos} show that the log-normal distribution offers the best fit, displacing the gamma and exponential distribution models. This transition from gamma to log-normal reflects the multiplicative nature of noise in strongly obstructed environments, where ranging error can be modeled as the product of attenuation and reflection factors rather than as simple additive Gaussian noise.

The profound deviation from normality under BS is mathematically critical. Figure \ref{fig:qq_nlos} presents the Quantile-Quantile (Q-Q) plot for the NLOS data against a theoretical Normal distribution. The extreme curvature and divergence in the upper tail confirm that large positive errors (biases) are vastly more frequent than a Gaussian model would predict. The Shapiro-Wilk normality test yielded $p \ll 0.001$, firmly rejecting the Gaussian hypothesis.

\subsection{Non-Parametric Statistical Testing}

Because the data severely violate the assumptions of normality and homoscedasticity (i.e., Levene's test, $p \ll 0.001$), standard parametric tests such as ANOVA are methodologically invalid for this dataset. Consequently, it was utilized the Mann-Whitney U test to quantify the magnitude of the differences between locations. The contrast between the best-performing placements (Head/Wrist) and the worst-performing placements (Hip/Chest) yielded an exceptionally large effect size (i.e., $r = 0.968$ in NLOS, $p \ll 0.001$), showing conclusively that node placement on the body is a dominant, statistically undeniable factor in determining the reliability of 6.5 GHz UWB systems.
