The experimental evaluation provides crucial empirical evidence regarding the performance of UWB systems operating in the 6.5 GHz band. Unlike previous studies focused on lower frequencies (3-5 GHz), our data reveals that the shorter wavelength of 6.5 GHz signals introduces specific vulnerability patterns to body shadowing.

\subsection{Phase 1: Validation Phase}
This phase focuses on the systematic quantification of ranging errors across seven body locations to establish a baseline for sensor placement.

\subsubsection{Quantification of BS Impact}
The comparative analysis of distance error by body location (Table \ref{tab:resumen_mae}) establishes a clear hierarchy of robustness for wearable tag placement. The head location emerges as the optimal placement, maintaining sub-decimeter accuracy (MAE < 9 cm) even in most indoor scenarios. This performance is attributed to the creeping wave diffraction phenomenon around the skull.

Conversely, the hip and chest locations exhibit critical degradation, with NLOS errors increasing by a factor of up to \textbf{13x} compared to LOS conditions (e.g., Hip: 7.22 cm in LOS vs 95.11 cm in NLOS). This result is significant as it quantifies the "cost" of convenient placement: placing the tag on the torso effectively renders standard ranging algorithms useless without advanced bias compensation.

A counter-intuitive but critical finding is the \textit{Constructive Multipath Effect} observed indoors. For locations like the head and hand, NLOS errors were actually \textbf{lower} in the indoor corridor (18.16 cm) compared to the open outdoor field (32.43 cm). This confirms that in confined 6.5 GHz environments, reflections from walls and ceilings can partially compensate for direct body blocking.

\subsubsection{Probabilistic Error Characterization}
Statistical error modeling is essential for designing robust navigation algorithms. Using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), the fit of various theoretical distributions (Gamma, Log-normal, Exponential, Normal) to empirical data was evaluated.

In LOS conditions, the Gamma distribution proved to be the optimal model (see Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_los}), adequately characterizing the slight positive skewness. However, in NLOS conditions, the error structure changes radically. Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_nlos} conclusively demonstrates that \textbf{the Log-normal distribution offers the best fit for NLOS data}, significantly outperforming Gamma. This finding reflects the multiplicative nature of noise in obstructed environments. Distance error in NLOS can be approximately expressed as: $d_{\text{error}} \propto d_{\text{real}} \cdot (1 + \alpha_1 \cdot \alpha_2 \cdot \alpha_3)$, where $\alpha_i$ represent multiplicative attenuation factors.

\subsection{Phase 2: Performance Phase (Positioning System Evaluation)}
This phase evaluates the dynamic performance of the system in a real-world scenario, analyzing the impact of body shadowing on signal stability and trajectory estimation.

\subsubsection{Analysis of Time of Flight (ToF) Stability}
Within the context of the positioning system evaluation, we analyzed the Time of Flight (ToF) distributions to visualize the signal behavior under real tracking conditions. This analysis explicitly demonstrates the variability introduced when the tag transitions between Line-of-Sight (LOS) and Non-Line-of-Sight (NLOS) relative to the fixed anchors.

As shown in Table \ref{tab:tof_stats} and Figure \ref{fig:tof_pecho_dist}, when the body obstructs the direct path to an anchor, the ToF variance increases significantly (Standard Deviation increases by 3.5x). This temporal dispersion explains the "noisy" nature of the range measurements observed during the positioning experiments.
