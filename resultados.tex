The experimental results demonstrate a strong dependence between device location, scenario, and propagation condition (LOS/NLOS).

Data analysis reveals that the head offers the best overall performance, with stable behavior and moderate degradation between LOS and NLOS (especially indoors, with a factor of 2.0x), possibly because the skull geometry facilitates diffraction. Regarding extremities, the wrist and hand show moderate and consistent performance, positioning them as viable alternatives to the head, while the ankle and knee record higher errors due to proximity to the ground and leg obstruction.

Complementarily, non-parametric statistical analysis confirmed that differences between configurations are statistically significant ($p \ll 0.001$). A relevant finding was the differential behavior between scenarios: for several locations (head, hand, ankle), NLOS error was lower indoors compared to outdoors. This phenomenon does not imply a real "improvement" in accuracy, but reflects a qualitative change in the propagation mechanism. In the indoor environment (corridor with nearby walls), the proximity of reflective surfaces allows maintaining signal connectivity through multiple paths (reflections) which, although introducing additional delays and temporal variability, guarantee continuous energy reception. In contrast, outdoors, when the body obstructs the direct path, the signal is severely attenuated without alternative nearby reflection sources, resulting in loss of signal integrity \cite{ref11, ref17}. This distinction between indoor (persistent multipath) and outdoor (absence of propagation alternatives) is critical for interpreting the nature of the error observed in Phase 2.

\subsection{Temporal Analysis of Time of Flight (ToF)}
Detailed analysis of Time of Flight (ToF) reveals critical patterns explaining both distance accuracy degradation and the probabilistic structure of the error. Signal temporal variability, quantified by standard deviation ($\sigma$), was identified as the most robust indicator to distinguish between LOS and NLOS conditions and inform the EKF covariance matrix configuration in Phase 2.

As observed in Table \ref{tab:tof_stats}, moving from a line-of-sight condition to body obstruction, ToF standard deviation increases drastically, with an average increase of 3.5$\times$ (from 0.18~ns to 0.63~ns), illustrating the exponential increase in temporal uncertainty under body shadowing. This variability increase is the temporal manifestation of the severe distance errors documented in Table \ref{tab:resumen_mae}.

\subsection{Probabilistic Error Characterization}
Statistical error modeling is essential for designing robust navigation algorithms. Using the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC), the fit of various theoretical distributions (Gamma, Log-normal, Exponential, Normal) to empirical data was evaluated, selecting the model that best captured the error tail structure.

In LOS conditions, the Gamma distribution proved to be the optimal model (see Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_los}), adequately characterizing the slight positive skewness inherent to ToF measurement in direct propagation. However, in NLOS conditions, the error structure changes radically. Figure \ref{fig:ajuste_nlos} conclusively demonstrates that \textbf{the Log-normal distribution offers the best fit for NLOS data}, significantly outperforming Gamma according to the AIC criterion. This finding is not accidental: it reflects the multiplicative nature of noise in obstructed environments. Unlike additive Gaussian errors assumed by classic linear filters, NLOS errors result from multiplicative processes where multiple factors (BS attenuation, multipath delay, diffraction distortion) concatenate, generating distributions with heavy tails characteristic of these processes. Distance error in NLOS can be approximately expressed as: $d_{\text{error}} \propto d_{\text{real}} \cdot (1 + \alpha_1 \cdot \alpha_2 \cdot \alpha_3)$, where $\alpha_i$ represent multiplicative attenuation factors, whose composition generates the observed Log-normal distribution.

Deviation from normality is critical for understanding the limitations of traditional filters. Figure \ref{fig:qq_nlos} presents the Q-Q plot for NLOS data, where extreme divergence in the upper tail confirms that large errors are much more frequent than a Gaussian distribution would predict (Shapiro-Wilk test: $p \ll 0.001$). Specifically, the pronounced curvature at the right extreme indicates that the probability of "large error events" in NLOS is orders of magnitude higher than under the Gaussian hypothesis, explaining why classic linear filters (Linear Kalman) are ineffective in the presence of severe body obstruction. The Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) employed in Phase 2, being informed by these empirical standard deviations (Table \ref{tab:tof_stats}) in its measurement covariance matrix configuration, provides an adaptation mechanism that, although not completely resolving non-Gaussianity, significantly improves trajectory coherence compared to a non-adaptive linear filter.

\subsection{2D Positioning System Evaluation}
To validate the impact of distance errors in a real application, a 2D positioning system was implemented in a 7.4 $\times$ 10.4 m hall, equipped with four fixed anchors in the corners. A test trajectory composed of 18 stop points (stationary stop of ~70 seconds at each point) was traced, distributed in a grid of 3 rows $\times$ 6 columns with 1.5~m spacing.

\subsubsection{Reference Case: Tag without Human Body}
Figure \ref{fig:kalman_tag} shows results for the reference \textit{tag} (without human body). The EKF (blue line) effectively smooths raw multilateration fluctuations (orange points), achieving a trajectory that faithfully fits the real reference (green points), with an average positioning error of 48 cm. This performance represents the optimal system case: when no body obstruction exists, measurement noise is approximately Gaussian with zero mean, and the EKF converges correctly to real positions.

\subsubsection{Case with Body Obstruction: Hip}
Introducing the human factor with the tag on the hip, degradation is drastic. Figure \ref{fig:kalman_cadera} illustrates this critical case. Significant deviations are appreciated at multiple trajectory points, with position errors reaching up to 95 cm. Although the Kalman filter (blue line) manages to smooth random fluctuations (reducing *jitter* compared to raw multilateration orange points), the filtered line consistently converges AWAY from the true position (green points).

This systematic separation between the smoothed trajectory and the real reference is not noise, it is BIAS. The fundamental problem is that the EKF assumes measurement noise with zero mean: $\mathbf{v}_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{R})$. However, under severe body obstruction, the obstruction introduces a systematic positive bias: every measured distance is ALWAYS greater than the real distance $d_{\text{measured}} = d_{\text{real}} + \text{bias}(\text{location})$. Since the filter lacks an external reference to estimate this bias in real-time, it converges towards the smoothed erroneous measurement, amplifying positional error. In other words: the EKF efficiently smooths an incorrect measurement.
